📖 About

Baisakhi falls every year on the fixed date of April 13th — a solar calendar festival marking the Sikh New Year. It celebrates three things simultaneously: the Rabi wheat harvest reaching maturity in Punjab; the birth of the Khalsa Panth on April 13, 1699; and the beginning of the Sikh solar New Year in the Nanakshahi calendar.

"On April 13, 1699, Guru Gobind Singh Ji gathered hundreds of thousands of Sikhs at Anandpur Sahib and called for five volunteers willing to give their heads for the Guru. After silence — five ordinary men stood up. He initiated them with Amrit, gave them the Five Ks, and transformed Sikhism forever. Every Baisakhi asks the same question: what are we willing to stand up for?"

⭐ Significance

Baisakhi is the day a Guru asked who would give their head — and five ordinary men stood up and became extraordinary. The Panj Pyare (Five Beloved Ones) were not warriors or scholars — they were ordinary people from different castes and regions who had one thing in common: complete faith and willingness to sacrifice. That willingness is what the Khalsa was born from. It is what Baisakhi asks us to remember every year.

🌅 Morning — Amrit Vela & Nitnem

✦ Before Dawn
Amrit Vela — Rise Before Dawn

Wake up before dawn — the ambrosial hours of 3–6 AM when the mind is at its quietest. Take a full Ishnaan (ritual bath). Wear clean traditional clothes — bright colors for women with a Phulkari dupatta, white kurta-pajama or blue for men. Cover your head. No leather items to the Gurudwara.

✦ Morning
Nitnem — The Five Daily Prayers

Recite the morning Nitnem with a Gutka Sahib or the iGurbani/SikhNet app for audio. Japji Sahib (Guru Nanak's foundational morning prayer) · Jaap Sahib (Guru Gobind Singh Ji's 199 names of God) · Tav Prasad Savaiye (rejection of empty ritual) · Chaupai Sahib (prayer of divine protection) · Anand Sahib (hymn of divine bliss). On Baisakhi morning, these prayers take on additional weight — Guru Gobind Singh Ji composed three of these five Banis himself.

✦ Morning
Ardas — The Sikh Supplication

After Nitnem, stand with folded hands and offer Ardas — the formal Sikh supplication that invokes all Ten Gurus and the Guru Granth Sahib Ji, remembers the Sikh martyrs, and asks for God's grace. On Baisakhi, include specific gratitude for the Panj Pyare and the founding of the Khalsa on this day. Conclude: "Waheguru Ji Ka Khalsa, Waheguru Ji Ki Fateh."

✦ Optional
Holy Dip — Sarovar or River

Taking a dip in a holy water body on Baisakhi morning is considered deeply auspicious — especially in the Amritsar sarovar (holy tank) at Harmandir Sahib (Golden Temple). The sarovar on Baisakhi is filled with thousands of devotees before dawn. If not possible, add a few drops of Gangajal to your morning bath.

🕌 At the Gurudwara

✦ Entry
Matha Tekna — Complete Humility

Remove shoes outside. Remove all leather items. Wash hands and feet. Cover head at all times. Enter quietly. The moment you see Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji — go on both knees and touch your forehead to the ground: Matha Tekna. Sit cross-legged facing the Granth. Never point feet toward the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. Never sit on a raised surface in the Darbar Sahib.

✦ Most Sacred Today
Akhand Path Bhog

Gurudwaras begin an Akhand Path (48-hour continuous recitation of the entire Guru Granth Sahib Ji — all 1430 pages) two days before Baisakhi, concluding on Baisakhi morning. Attend the Bhog ceremony — the conclusion. It is followed by Ardas, Hukamnama (the Guru's word for the day), and Karah Prasad distribution. The Bhog of an Akhand Path on Baisakhi is the most important moment of the day.

✦ All Day
Kirtan — Listen & Absorb

Sit in the Sangat and listen to Kirtan — devotional singing of Gurbani in classical Ragas. On Baisakhi, Kirtan particularly focuses on Shabads celebrating the creation of the Khalsa and Guru Gobind Singh Ji's courage. Even sitting quietly absorbing the sound of Gurbani sung in Ragas is a complete act of worship. Close your eyes. Let it wash over you.

✦ Receive
Karah Prasad — Both Hands Cupped

When sewadars come with the warm halwa — cup both hands together, right over left. Receive with humility. Eat immediately. Made of equal parts whole wheat flour, sugar, and ghee — Guru's blessing made edible, representing the equality of all people before the Guru. On Baisakhi, the Karah Prasad is offered after the Bhog of the Akhand Path — receive it as the conclusion of forty-eight hours of prayer.

✦ Essential Seva
Langar Seva — Cook, Serve, Eat

Volunteer in the langar kitchen — chopping vegetables, making rotis, washing dishes, or serving. On Baisakhi, Gurudwaras feed thousands. Arrive early and tell a sewadar you wish to do langar seva. Then eat langar yourself sitting on the floor in Pangat (rows) with everyone else — the same simple food served with love, shared equally. Langar seva on Baisakhi is among the highest forms of worship on this day.

✦ Historic
Amrit Sanchar — Taking Amrit

The most significant Baisakhi event: the Amrit Sanchar — formal initiation into the Khalsa. Five initiated Sikhs representing the Panj Pyare prepare Amrit (water stirred in an iron bowl with a Khanda while reciting the five Banis). Amrit is sprinkled on the initiate's eyes and hair and drunk five times. The initiate commits to the Five Ks and the Khalsa way of life. If you are a Sikh ready to take Amrit — Baisakhi is the most auspicious day of the year to do so.

⚔️ The Five Ks — Given to the Khalsa on the First Baisakhi
These five articles of faith were given by Guru Gobind Singh Ji to the Panj Pyare on April 13, 1699 — and every Khalsa Sikh wears them from the day of Amrit initiation.
💇
Kesh — Uncut Hair
Accepting God's creation as-is. Covered by the Dastar — a crown of spirituality and sovereignty.
⭕
Kara — Steel Bracelet
A circle with no beginning or end — God's infinite nature. Every hand movement a reminder to act righteously.
🪮
Kanga — Wooden Comb
Cleanliness, discipline, keeping order in hair and mind. Worn in the hair at all times.
🩳
Kachera — Cotton Undergarment
Self-restraint and modesty. A warrior's garment — allowing free movement with dignity.
🗡️
Kirpan — Steel Sword
Commitment to defend the weak. Not aggression — the strong's responsibility to protect those who cannot protect themselves.

🥁 Nagar Kirtan & the Harvest Celebration

✦ Procession
Nagar Kirtan — Through the Streets

A grand procession moves through the streets — led by the Panj Pyare in traditional blue Khalsa warrior attire. The Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji is carried on a decorated Palki Sahib, surrounded by reverence. Thousands of Sangat members follow on foot chanting Gurbani. Ragis sing Kirtan continuously. Walk in the procession with folded hands. As a spectator, stand and bow your head as the Granth passes.

✦ Martial Art
Gatka — Sikh Martial Art

Trained youth perform Gatka — the Sikh martial art — with swords, spears, and traditional weapons along the Nagar Kirtan route. This is not performance but a living demonstration of the warrior spirit Guru Gobind Singh Ji embedded in the Khalsa. The movements are precise, powerful, and spiritually charged. Baisakhi is the most important Gatka occasion of the year.

✦ Harvest Joy
Bhangra & Gidda — Dance the Harvest

In Punjab's villages and city melas alike — the dhol beats and Bhangra begins: vigorous, full-body, arms raised, jumping in rhythm. Women perform Gidda — graceful, clapping, spinning, singing witty Boliyan verses. The wheat crop is in. The Khalsa was born. The new year begins. These three truths together demand dancing. Join in without hesitation — Baisakhi without Bhangra is incomplete.

✦ Community
Baisakhi Mela — Festival Fair

Across Punjab and in cities with significant Punjabi communities — Baisakhi melas (fairs) are organized with Bhangra and Gidda performances, folk music, traditional food stalls (makki di roti, sarson da saag, lassi, pinni), handicraft displays, and cultural programs. Attend one if available — they carry the living culture of Punjab into the present.

🗺️ Where Baisakhi Is Celebrated

Harmandir Sahib, Amritsar
The Golden Temple — Most Sacred Site

The Golden Temple complex transforms on Baisakhi — the sarovar (holy tank) full of pilgrims before dawn, the Akhand Path Bhog at morning, the Nagar Kirtan through Amritsar's streets, and Amrit Sanchar ceremonies through the day. If you are Sikh and within reach of Amritsar — Baisakhi here is the most complete possible celebration.

Anandpur Sahib, Punjab
Birthplace of the Khalsa

The actual site where Guru Gobind Singh Ji created the Khalsa on April 13, 1699. On Baisakhi, lakhs gather here for a three-day Hola Mohalla festival that includes Gatka demonstrations, Kirtan, Langar, and Nagar Kirtan. The spiritual weight of standing where the Khalsa was born is immeasurable.

Punjab's Villages & Fields
The Harvest Root

In the villages of Punjab — where the wheat fields turn golden in April — Baisakhi is felt most organically. Farmers offer prayers of gratitude for the harvest, communities gather in the fields for Bhangra, and the connection between the earth's abundance and the Guru's grace is most directly felt.

Delhi, Mumbai & Diaspora
Khalsa Celebrations Worldwide

Every city with a Sikh community — from Delhi to London to Vancouver — holds Baisakhi Nagar Kirtan and Gurudwara celebrations. The Baisakhi parade in Vancouver is one of the largest outside India. The Khalsa was born in Punjab but belongs to humanity — Baisakhi is celebrated wherever Sikhs have carried their faith.

Baisakhi as Puthandu & Vishu
New Year Across India

April 13th is also New Year across several Indian traditions — Puthandu in Tamil Nadu, Vishu in Kerala, Bihu in Assam, Pohela Boishakh in Bengal. The same solar alignment that makes it Sikh New Year marks new beginnings across cultures — a day when India begins again.

🙏 Key Prayers & Gurbani

✨
Mool Mantar — The Root Mantra of Sikhism (Japji Sahib opening)
"Ik Onkar, Sat Naam, Karta Purakh, Nirbhau, Nirvair, Akaal Moorat, Ajooni Saibhang, Gur Prasad. Jap."
One God. Truth is His name. Creator. Fearless. Without enmity. Timeless. Beyond birth and death. Self-illuminated. Realized by Guru's grace. The foundational statement of Sikh theology — every recitation of Japji Sahib begins here. On Baisakhi, this is the first thing spoken after rising.
⚔️
Deh Shiva Bar Mohe — The Khalsa Warrior's Prayer by Guru Gobind Singh Ji
"Deh Shiva Bar Mohe Ihai, Shubh Karman Te Kabhu Na Tarou"
O God, grant me this boon — may I never hesitate from righteous deeds. May I never fear the enemy in battle. May I be victorious. When the battle is over, may I sing Your praises. Composed by Guru Gobind Singh Ji — the prayer of the Khalsa warrior. On Baisakhi, the day the Khalsa was born, this prayer is especially powerful.
🌟
Sikh Greeting — Said to Every Sikh Today
"Waheguru Ji Ka Khalsa, Waheguru Ji Ki Fateh"
The Khalsa belongs to the Wondrous Lord. Victory belongs to the Wondrous Lord. Given by Guru Gobind Singh Ji himself — both people say it to each other. A battle cry and a greeting in one phrase. On Baisakhi — the birthday of the Khalsa — say this to every Sikh you encounter. It is the most appropriate Baisakhi greeting.
🕊️
Waheguru Simran — Continuous Throughout the Day
"Waheguru, Waheguru, Waheguru..."
Wondrous Lord — inhale "Wahe," exhale "Guru." The Guru said Simran (continuous remembrance of God's name) is the highest spiritual practice — everything else is secondary. Use your Tasbih mala to count. On Baisakhi — whether at the Gurudwara, in the Nagar Kirtan procession, or going about your day — let Waheguru run beneath every moment.

📌 Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do's

  • Wake at Amrit Vela and recite full Nitnem
  • Cover head at all times in the Gurudwara
  • Perform Matha Tekna before Guru Granth Sahib Ji
  • Attend the Akhand Path Bhog — the most important ceremony of the day
  • Do langar seva — volunteer in the kitchen without fail
  • Walk in or respectfully observe the Nagar Kirtan
  • Greet every Sikh with "Waheguru Ji Ka Khalsa, Waheguru Ji Ki Fateh"
  • Dance Bhangra and Gidda — the harvest and the Khalsa both demand it
  • If you are Sikh and ready — consider taking Amrit on this most auspicious day
  • Do continuous Waheguru Simran throughout the day

✕ Don'ts

  • No leather items in the Gurudwara — this is a strict rule always
  • Never sit with feet pointing toward the Guru Granth Sahib Ji
  • Never turn your back to the Guru Granth Sahib Ji when leaving — step backward while facing the Guru
  • Do not speak loudly or use your phone during Kirtan in the Darbar Sahib
  • Do not treat Baisakhi as only a harvest party or Punjabi cultural event — its spiritual and historical significance is immense
  • Do not waste langar food — receive only what you will eat, eat with gratitude

He asked who would give their head. After silence, five ordinary men stood. He gave them Amrit. He asked them to give him Amrit in return — making himself equal to his disciples. That humility and that courage, together, created the Khalsa. That is what we remember today.
