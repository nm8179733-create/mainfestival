📖 About

Onam celebrates the annual homecoming of the beloved demon king Mahabali (Maveli) — the most just and generous ruler Kerala has ever known — who comes back once a year to visit his people.

The Story: King Mahabali ruled a golden age — no poverty, no inequality, no dishonesty, no disease. Everyone was equal and happy. His greatness threatened the Gods, who asked Lord Vishnu to intervene. Vishnu came as Vamana — a tiny brahmin dwarf — and asked Mahabali for three paces of land. The generous king agreed. Vamana grew to cosmic proportions and covered the earth and sky in two steps. With the third, he pushed Mahabali into the netherworld. But so moved was Vishnu by Mahabali's virtue that he granted him one boon — to visit his beloved people once a year.

That annual visit is Onam. Every year Kerala prepares for Mahabali's arrival with flowers, feasts, and festivities — showing him that his people are still happy and prosperous.

"Onam is Kerala's most beautiful answer to the question — what does a just and happy society look like? It looks like Mahabali's kingdom. And every year, for ten days, Kerala tries to become that kingdom again — with flowers at every door, food for everyone, and the belief that a good king always comes home to his people."

⭐ Significance

Onam is Kerala's national festival — celebrated by Hindus, Christians, and Muslims alike. It belongs to every Keralite regardless of religion. The story of Mahabali is not just mythology — it is a political and moral ideal. A king so good, so just, so equal in his treatment of all people, that even a God had to trick him to remove him. Every year, Onam asks: are we still living up to the standard Mahabali set? The flowers, the feast, and the joy are Kerala's annual answer — yes, we still try.

🌺 Pookalam — The Flower Carpet

10 Rings · 10 Days · One for Each Day

Start with a small circle of flowers on Day 1 (Atham). Each day add one more ring outward — by Thiruvonam (Day 10) you have 10 concentric rings of different colored flowers: a magnificent living carpet at your door.

Place the Thrikkakara Appan clay idol at the center — the small pyramid representing Vamana/Mahabali — and perform daily puja around it. The flowers are the offering. The beauty of the Pookalam is the prayer.

🎊 Thiruvonam — The 10th and Greatest Day

✦ Dawn
Wake Early & Wear Kasavu

Wake before sunrise. Bathe. Wear Kasavu — Kerala's traditional white with gold border. Kasavu saree for women, Mundu and shirt for men. This is non-negotiable on Thiruvonam. Every Keralite wears Kasavu on this day — it is the most beautiful regional dress tradition in India.

✦ Morning
The Grand Pookalam

Make the grandest Pookalam of all — add the 10th ring, completing the full circular carpet. Decorate the entrance with banana leaves and mango leaf torans. Perform the Thrikkakara Appan puja with special devotion — this is the day Mahabali himself is believed to arrive.

✦ Heart
Onam Sadhya — The Grand Feast

The Sadhya is the most magnificent vegetarian feast in all of Indian cuisine. Served on a fresh banana leaf, sitting on the floor, with 26 or more dishes in a specific centuries-old arrangement. Eaten with the right hand only — no cutlery. Begin cooking at 4–5 AM.

✦ Tradition
The Banana Leaf — Fold Toward You

After eating, always fold the top half of the banana leaf toward you. Folding away signals dissatisfaction — a serious social signal you do not want to give your host. The folding toward you is a gesture of gratitude and joy for the meal.

✦ Community
Visit Family & Give Onakkodi

Thiruvonam is a day of family reunion — people travel from across India and the world to be in Kerala. Give Onakkodi (new clothes, traditionally Kasavu) to children and younger family members. The eldest gives to all younger members. Keep your home open — every visitor is welcomed and fed.

✦ Remember
Sing the Maveli Song

Sing — or at least know — the Maveli folk song. It is the soul of Onam. It describes Mahabali's golden age of equality and justice. "Maveli Nadu Vaneedum Kalam, Manusharellarum Onnu Pole" — when Maveli ruled, all people were equal. That longing is what Onam truly celebrates.

🍌 Onam Sadhya — 26 Dishes on a Banana Leaf

The Sadhya is served on a fresh banana leaf placed with the tip pointing left. Every dish has a specific position — centuries of tradition encoded in the arrangement. Here is the order of service, from first to last.

Before rice — placed first
Pappadum Ripe Banana Sharkaravaretti Upperi (Chips) Naranga Achar Puli Inji
Rice served — then curries come around
Kerala Red Rice Sambar Avial ✦ Thoran Olan
Kalan Pulissery Pachadi Erissery Koottukari Rasam Ghee
Payasam — served last, one by one
Palada Payasam ✦ Parippu Payasam Semiya Payasam

Can't cook all 26? Make at minimum: Avial, Sambar, Thoran, Olan, Pulissery, and Palada Payasam. These five constitute the essential Sadhya. If cooking at home is not possible — every Kerala restaurant across India serves full Onam Sadhya on Thiruvonam. Eating Sadhya on a banana leaf on Thiruvonam is the one non-negotiable celebration of the day.

🍽️ Key Foods of Onam

🥘
Avial — The Signature Dish
Mixed vegetables (raw banana, drumstick, yam, beans, raw mango) in ground coconut and curd with coconut oil and curry leaves. Every Kerala family has its own treasured Avial recipe. No Sadhya is complete without it.
🍮
Palada Payasam
The most sacred Onam sweet — thin rice flakes cooked slowly in full-fat milk with sugar for hours until thick and fragrant. The patience of the cooking is itself part of the tradition.
🫙
Puli Inji
Ginger-tamarind-jaggery chutney — served first on the banana leaf before anything else. Its sharp, complex flavors prepare the palate for the entire feast that follows.
🫘
Olan
Ash gourd and cowpeas in delicate coconut milk — subtle, pale, and gentle. The quiet dish of the Sadhya. Its simplicity is deceptive — it is many people's favorite.
🍌
Upperi (Banana Chips)
Kerala's famous thick banana chips fried in coconut oil — the evergreen Onam snack. The best ones are made at home. Ubiquitous across the 10-day festival season.
🥛
Pulissery
Ripe mango or pineapple in sweetened curd and coconut — sweet, sour, and cooling. The perfect counter to the richer dishes of the Sadhya.

🐘 Traditional Arts & Events

🚣
Vallamkali — Snake Boat Race
100-foot-long snake boats (Chundan Vallam) carrying 100+ rowers racing on Kerala's backwaters. The Nehru Trophy Boat Race at Punnamada Lake, Alappuzha is the most famous — watching it live is one of the greatest spectacles in Indian sporting and cultural life.
💃
Kaikottikali — Clapping Dance
Women in Kasavu sarees dance in gentle circles with clapping, singing Onam folk songs. A graceful, joyful expression of the festival's communal spirit — performed in homes, schools, and public spaces across Kerala.
🐯
Pulikali — Tiger Play
Men painted elaborately as tigers and leopards dance through the streets on Thiruvonam in Thrissur. One of the most visually stunning and joyful folk art performances of Kerala — impossible to watch without laughing and marveling.
🎭
Kathakali Performances
Classical Kerala dance-drama performances are held across the state during Onam season — elaborate costumes, codified gestures, and stories from the Mahabharata and Ramayana performed through the night.
🌸
Pookalam Competitions
Schools, offices, apartment complexes, and communities hold Pookalam competitions with prizes for the most beautiful, most creative, and largest designs. Even outside Kerala, wherever Keralites live, Pookalam competitions on Onam are a beloved tradition.
🛍️
Onam Shopping Season
The 10 days of Onam are Kerala's biggest shopping season — new clothes, gold jewelry, household items. Kerala's markets transform completely. The gifting of Kasavu to family members is itself a cherished tradition.

🙏 Prayers & Song of Onam

🎵
The Maveli Song — Soul of Onam · Sing This on Thiruvonam
"Maveli Nadu Vaneedum Kalam
Manusharellarum Onnu Pole
Amodhathode Vasikkum Kalam
Aapathangarkkum Ottum Kale"
When Maveli (Mahabali) ruled the land, all people were equal. They lived with joy — there was no sorrow for anyone. This folk song is the anthem of Onam, sung by every Keralite. It is a description of Mahabali's golden age — and a longing for that justice and equality to return.
🌺
Pookalam Puja — Daily Morning Prayer
"The flowers themselves are the prayer. The beauty of the Pookalam is the offering."
Light a diya and incense. Offer fresh flowers to the Thrikkakara Appan idol. Apply kumkum. Fold hands and pray — thanking Mahabali for his goodness, and asking that his spirit of justice and generosity continue to inspire your home. No fixed mantra — sincerity is enough.
🍌
Before Sadhya — Simple Gratitude
"Fold hands before the banana leaf. Close your eyes. Offer thanks."
Thank God, thank Mahabali, thank the hands that cooked, thank the harvest, thank the family gathered together. No fixed words are required — only sincerity. Then eat together with joy.

📌 Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do's

  • Make Pookalam every single day — even a small one. Don't skip a day.
  • Wear Kasavu (white and gold Kerala traditional dress) on Thiruvonam without fail
  • Eat Onam Sadhya on a banana leaf, sitting on the floor — this is the heart of the festival
  • Fold the banana leaf toward you after eating — always
  • Cook or participate in cooking the Sadhya — the labor of love is part of the celebration
  • Sing the Maveli song — it is the soul of Onam
  • Give Onakkodi (new clothes) to children and younger family members
  • Watch or attend Vallamkali (snake boat race) if you are in Kerala
  • Visit family — Thiruvonam is Kerala's great homecoming

✕ Don'ts

  • Do not skip the Pookalam — it is the daily ritual that holds all 10 days together
  • Do not eat Sadhya with cutlery — use your right hand, as tradition demands
  • Do not fold the banana leaf away from you after eating — it signals dissatisfaction
  • Do not wear any color other than Kasavu on Thiruvonam if you wish to observe fully
  • Do not treat Onam as just a food festival — understand Mahabali's story and what it means about justice, equality, and good governance
  • Do not let the day pass without at least singing the Maveli song — even once

Every year, for ten days, Kerala tries to become Mahabali's kingdom again — with flowers at every door, food for everyone, and the belief that a good king always comes home to his people.
