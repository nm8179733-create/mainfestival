✦ Why This Day

The Sun's Sacred Transit

☀

Makara means Capricorn. Sankranti means transition. On this day, the Sun (Surya) moves from Sagittarius into Capricorn — beginning its northward journey called Uttarayana. This is one of Hinduism's most astronomically precise rituals: the actual movement of the sun tracked and celebrated since Vedic times.

Uttarayana is considered the most auspicious six-month period of the year. The great warrior Bhishma Pitamaha lay wounded on his bed of arrows, refusing to breathe his last — waiting for Uttarayana to begin before he departed. Such is the spiritual gravity of this day: death during Uttarayana is believed to lead directly to moksha.

Unlike almost every other Hindu festival, Makar Sankranti follows the solar calendar — it falls on January 14th every year, fixed and predictable as the sun itself. It is celebrated across India under different names but with the same luminous heart: gratitude to the Sun for warmth, light, and the harvest.

🗓️

Always January 14th

One of the few Hindu festivals on a fixed solar date — not lunar. The solar calendar anchors it to the actual astronomical event.

🌅

Beginning of Uttarayana

The sun's northward journey begins. Days grow longer. Winter loosens its grip. The cosmos itself shifts direction on this day.

🌾

Pan-India Harvest

Pongal in Tamil Nadu · Lohri in Punjab · Bihu in Assam · Uttarayan in Gujarat · Sakraat in Rajasthan — one festival, many names.

☀️

Sun Worship at Its Peak

The Gayatri Mantra — humanity's greatest hymn to the Sun — is chanted at dawn while water is offered to the rising Surya.

The Sun is the soul of the world, the eye of the universe. When it moves northward it blesses all of creation — and the wise, knowing this, bathe, worship, give, and celebrate.

— Mahabharata, Anushasana Parva

✦ From Dawn to Dusk

How to Celebrate Makar Sankranti

🔥

Day Before — January 13th

Bhogi — Letting Go of the Old

Before sunrise, family members gather and burn old, broken, and unused items — worn clothes, cracked furniture, dried leaves — in a bonfire called Bhogi Mantalu. As the sun begins a new journey, you shed everything old from your life. Neighbors gather around the fire together. Children are showered with Bhogi Pallu — regu fruits, sugarcane, flowers, coins, and small toys poured over their heads as a blessing for health and long life.

🛁

Before Sunrise — Sankranti Morning

Sesame Oil Bath

Apply warm sesame (til) oil all over the body, massage thoroughly, then bathe with warm water. Sesame generates body heat (critical in January), is sacred to the Sun God, and is believed on this day to cleanse all sins accumulated over the past year. Wear new clothes after bathing — bright yellow, green, or orange.

☀️

At Sunrise — Most Sacred Ritual

Surya Arghya — Offering Water to the Sun

Go outside. Face East (non-negotiable for Sun worship). Fill a copper vessel with clean water. Add a pinch of kumkum, a few sesame seeds, a small flower, and turmeric. Hold the vessel above your head and pour a thin stream toward the sun, letting the water fall to the earth. The sunlight passes through the falling water — this is the offering. The water acts as a prism; the divine light enters your life through it.

"Om Suryaya Namaha · Om Mitraya Namaha · Om Adityaya Namaha"
— chant 3, 7, or 11 times while pouring
🪔

Morning Puja

Surya Puja & Chakra Pongal Naivedyam

Set up the puja room with Kalash, Surya idol, and puja thali. Offer sesame and jaggery — the signature sacred combination — to God before eating them yourself. Offer sugarcane and prepare Chakra Pongal (sweet rice with jaggery, moong dal, ghee, and cardamom) as naivedyam. In some traditions, Pongal is cooked outdoors in a new earthen pot until it overflows — the overflow symbolizing abundance.

🤝

After Puja — Community

Distributing Til Ladoo to Neighbors

Go door to door and give sesame-jaggery ladoos to every household in your neighborhood. In Maharashtra, you say aloud: "Til-Gul Ghya, God God Bola" — "Take sesame and jaggery, speak sweet words." This is one of India's most beautiful social traditions: the giving of something sweet paired with a reminder to be kind, forgive past bitterness, and enter the new solar year with sweetness in words and heart.

"Til-Gul Ghya, God God Bola"
🐄

Third Day — Kanuma

Cattle Worship — Goru Puja

In Telugu and Kannada traditions, the third day is Kanuma — dedicated to the cattle who made the harvest possible. Cows and bulls are bathed, their horns painted in bright colors, adorned with flower garlands, bells, and cloth. Kumkum and turmeric mark their foreheads. They are fed jaggery, pongal, and sugarcane. The farmer and family bow before them. Even in cities, visiting and feeding a cow at a gosala on this day is deeply auspicious.

✦ The Sky Tradition

Kite Flying — Why the Skies Fill with Color

🪁

The tradition of flying kites on Sankranti is connected to the early morning winter sun. In January, morning sunlight is gentle, healing, and rich in Vitamin D. Ayurveda prescribed long hours under this early sun for skin health, energy, and wellbeing. Kite flying was the cultural mechanism that ensured people spent hours outdoors absorbing this beneficial light.

The sky above Indian cities on Sankranti is one of the most spectacular sights in the world — hundreds of colorful diamond kites against a winter blue sky, strings crossing, battles erupting, kites falling and rising. When you cut someone's kite string, the crowd erupts:

"Kai Po Che!"

— Gujarati for "I have cut it!" A joyful shout heard across rooftops across India.

✦ The Festival Table

Traditional Sankranti Foods

🍚

Chakra Pongal

Sweet Harvest Rice

Rice and moong dal cooked with jaggery, ghee, cardamom, and cashews. Boiled in a new earthen pot until it overflows — the overflow symbolizes abundance.

🟤

Til Ladoo

Sesame-Jaggery Balls

The festival's most iconic sweet — toasted sesame seeds bound together with melted jaggery, rolled into warm golden balls and distributed to all neighbors.

🫓

Ariselu

Adhirasam — Rice Jaggery Discs

Deep-fried sweet discs of rice flour and jaggery — crisp outside, soft within. Prepared in large batches for the festival season and stored for weeks.

🫛

Pesarattu

Green Moong Crepes

Andhra's beloved savory breakfast — thin crispy crepes of soaked green moong dal served with ginger-coconut chutney and a mound of upma inside.

🍮

Boorelu

Stuffed Sweet Dumplings

Steamed or fried outer shell stuffed with a sweet chana dal and jaggery mixture. A traditional Andhra Sankranti sweet found in every household.

🍚

Pulihora

Tamarind Rice

Tangy tamarind rice tempered with mustard, dry red chilies, peanuts, and curry leaves. The quintessential Telugu festival rice — served on banana leaf.

✦ Sacred Prayers

Mantras of Makar Sankranti

Surya Mantra — Arghya

Om Hraam Hreem Hraum
Sah Suryaya Namaha

The beej (seed) mantra of Lord Surya — charged with the solar energy of Hraam, Hreem, Hraum. Chant while offering water at sunrise.

Gayatri Mantra — The Great Sun Prayer

Om Bhur Bhuvah Swaha
Tat Savitur Varenyam
Bhargo Devasya Dhimahi
Dhiyo Yo Nah Prachodayat

"We meditate on the glorious light of the divine Sun. May that light illuminate our minds and guide our intellect." — Humanity's most ancient and powerful solar hymn.

Simple Gratitude — Surya Namaskara

Om Suryaya Namaha
Om Adityaya Namaha
Om Bhaskaraya Namaha

"Salutations to the Sun · to the first light (Aditya) · to the one who illuminates (Bhaskara)." Three names, three aspects, one divine solar presence.

✦ Sacred Guidelines

Do's & Don'ts of Makar Sankranti

✓

Do's

  • ☀️

    Take sesame oil bath without fail

    Apply warm til oil all over the body before bathing. Sesame generates heat in winter, is sacred to Surya, and is believed to cleanse the year's accumulated sins.

  • 🌅

    Offer Surya Arghya at sunrise — facing East

    The most sacred ritual of the day. Pour water mixed with kumkum, sesame, and turmeric toward the rising sun while chanting Surya mantras. Non-negotiable direction: East.

  • 🟤

    Eat til (sesame) and jaggery

    The sacred food combination of this festival. Heat-generating in winter, sacred to the Sun God. Always offer to God before consuming.

  • 🤝

    Give Til Ladoo to neighbors and family

    Go door to door with sesame-jaggery balls. Say "Til-Gul Ghya, God God Bola." Let sweetness in food become sweetness in relationships and words.

  • 🕌

    Visit temple and seek blessings

    Uttarayana's first day is exceptionally auspicious. Prayers, offerings, and temple visits on this day carry multiplied spiritual merit.

  • 🎁

    Donate food, blankets, or sesame-jaggery to the needy

    Charity given on Uttarayana's first day is believed to return manifold blessings throughout the year. Giving is integral to this festival's spirit.

  • 🪁

    Fly kites and spend time outdoors in the morning sun

    January's early sunlight is gentle and healing. Spend maximum time under it. The tradition of kite flying exists precisely to keep people joyfully outdoors in this beneficial light.

  • 👨‍👩‍👧

    Celebrate with family and community

    Bhogi bonfire with neighbors, Til Ladoo distribution, kite flying together — Makar Sankranti is deeply communal. Its joy multiplies when shared.

✗

Don'ts

  • 🚫

    Do not consume non-vegetarian food

    Traditional practice on Sankranti day is to keep the body and home sattvic (pure). Non-vegetarian food is avoided as this is a day of Sun worship and spiritual cleansing.

  • ✂️

    Avoid using sharp objects unnecessarily

    Traditional belief holds that cutting with scissors or knives unnecessarily on this auspicious day can symbolically "cut" good fortune. Essential cooking use is fine — unnecessary cutting is avoided.

  • 🗣️

    Do not speak harshly

    "God God Bola" — speak sweetly — applies to the entire day and the entire season. Sankranti is a time of renewal; harsh words, arguments, and bitterness are to be left behind with the old year.

  • 🍱

    Do not waste food

    This is a harvest festival — food is the direct gift of the Sun, earth, and the farmers who labored. Wasting food on this day is considered deeply inauspicious and disrespectful to the harvest's spirit.

  • 😴

    Do not sleep through sunrise

    Surya Arghya must be performed at or just after sunrise. Sleeping in and missing the rising sun defeats the central purpose of the day — honoring Surya at the moment of his northward journey.

  • 🧨

    Avoid glass-coated kite string (manja)

    Traditional glass-coated manja is now banned in many states due to injuries to people, birds, and motorcyclists. Use cotton or polyester string — celebrate joyfully and safely.
