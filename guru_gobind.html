📖 About

Guru Gobind Singh Ji was the Tenth and last human Sikh Guru — born in Patna Sahib on December 22, 1666 to Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji and Mata Gujri Ji. He was simultaneously a saint of deepest spiritual devotion, a warrior of fearless courage, a poet of sublime beauty, a philosopher of profound wisdom, and a father who sacrificed everything for righteousness.

Sacrifice of his father: When Gobind was 9, a delegation of Kashmiri Pandits came to his father weeping — Aurangzeb was forcing mass conversions. The young Gobind told his father there was no greater soul to stand for the oppressed. Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji went to Delhi, refused to convert, and was publicly beheaded. He gave his life to protect the religious freedom of people of a different faith.

"When the news came that his two youngest sons — Zorawar Singh (9) and Fateh Singh (7) — had been bricked alive within a wall for refusing to convert, Guru Gobind Singh Ji is said to have said: 'The sparrows have killed the hawks — the young lions have shown the path of righteousness.' He did not break. He continued his mission."

Before his passing in 1708, he declared that after him there would be no human Guru — he installed the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji as the eternal, living Guru of the Sikhs for all time. This is why Sikhs treat the scripture not as a book but as a living Guru.

⭐ Significance

This Jayanti is not just a birthday celebration. It is a day to remember that one human being, through absolute faith in the divine and absolute love for humanity, can transform the course of history. He gave his father, his sons, his comfort, his home — everything — and never once wavered. On this day we don't just remember him. We ask ourselves: what are we willing to give?

⚔️ Creation of the Khalsa — Baisakhi 1699

On April 13, 1699 at Anandpur Sahib, Guru Gobind Singh Ji called together hundreds of thousands of Sikhs. He came out of his tent holding a drawn sword and asked: "Who will give their head for the Guru?" After a silence, one by one, five men came forward willing to sacrifice their lives. These five — the Panj Pyare (Five Beloved Ones) — became the nucleus of the Khalsa Panth.

He gave them Amrit (nectar) in a steel bowl stirred with a double-edged sword (Khanda) while reciting Gurbani. He then asked the five to give him Amrit in return — making himself equal to his disciples. An act of supreme humility from a Guru to his Sikhs.

"He gave all Sikh men the surname Singh (lion) and women the surname Kaur (princess) — eliminating caste distinctions in a single stroke. The Five Ks were given. A new people were born."

🔱 The Five Kakars — Khalsa Symbols

💇
Kesh — Uncut Hair
Accepting God's creation as it is. Kesh covered by a dastar (turban) represents sovereignty, dignity, and the crown of spirituality.
⭕
Kara — Steel Bracelet
Worn on the right wrist — a circle with no beginning and no end, representing God's infinite nature. Every time your hand moves, you are reminded to act righteously.
🪮
Kanga — Wooden Comb
Cleanliness, discipline, and the importance of keeping order — in hair, mind, and life. Kept in the hair at all times.
🩳
Kachera — Cotton Undergarment
Self-restraint, modesty, and readiness — a warrior's garment that allows free movement while maintaining dignity at all times.
🗡️
Kirpan — Steel Sword
The commitment to defend the weak and stand against injustice. Not a weapon of aggression — a symbol of the responsibility of the strong to protect those who cannot protect themselves.

🎭 How to Observe the Jayanti — Day by Day

✦ 48 Hours Before
Akhand Path Begins

The continuous, uninterrupted recitation of the entire Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji — all 1430 pages — without a single break, taking ~48 hours. Teams of Granthis rotate day and night. It is timed to conclude on the morning of the Jayanti. Visit the Gurudwara for the Bhog (conclusion) ceremony — the spiritual climax of the celebration.

✦ 2–6 AM
Amrit Vela — Rise Before Dawn

The ambrosial predawn hours — when the mind is quietest and connection to the divine most accessible. Guru Gobind Singh Ji himself woke at Amrit Vela every day. On his Jayanti, waking at this hour is the first act of honoring his discipline. Wash hands and face. Come to a clean sitting place in silence.

✦ Morning
Ishnaan — Ritual Bath

Take a full clean bath — Ishnaan — before morning prayers. The body must be clean before the mind engages with the divine. After bathing, wear clean traditional clothes. Men cover head with dastar or patka. Women cover head with dupatta. Come to the prayer space clean and ready.

✦ Morning
Nitnem — Five Morning Banis

Sit cross-legged with your Gutka Sahib. Recite all five morning Banis clearly and with understanding: Japji Sahib, Jaap Sahib (the Guru's own composition — 199 names of God), Tav Prasad Savaiye, Chaupai Sahib (the Guru's prayer of protection), and Anand Sahib. ~45–60 minutes. Use iGurbani or SikhNet app for audio guidance.

"Hamri karo haath dai rachha, pooran hoye chit ki ichha" — Chaupai Sahib
✦ Morning
Ardas — Prayer of Supplication

Stand with hands folded and perform Ardas — invoking all Ten Gurus and the Guru Granth Sahib Ji, remembering Sikh martyrs, and asking for God's grace. On this Jayanti, Ardas includes specific thanks for Guru Gobind Singh Ji's life and sacrifice. Conclude with:

"Waheguru Ji Ka Khalsa, Waheguru Ji Ki Fateh"
✦ Morning
Hukamnama — The Divine Order

After Ardas, take the Hukamnama — a random opening of Guru Granth Sahib Ji to receive the Guru's word for the day. If you do not have the Granth at home, read the daily Hukamnama from Darbar Sahib (Golden Temple) at sgpc.net or the SikhNet app. On this Jayanti, the Darbar Sahib Hukamnama carries special significance — read it, reflect, carry its message.

✦ Main Act
Visit the Gurudwara — Matha Tekna

The central act of the day. Remove shoes. Wash hands. Cover head. Enter the Darbar Sahib — the moment you see the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji, go on both knees and place your forehead on the floor: Matha Tekna — complete humility before the Guru. Sit cross-legged facing the Granth. Do not point feet toward it. Never sit on a raised surface in the Darbar Sahib.

✦ Gurudwara
Listen to Kirtan & Attend Bhog

Ragis sing Gurbani in classical Ragas from early morning through the day. Sit, listen, and absorb — even without understanding Punjabi, the sound of Gurbani in Ragas is a profound experience. Close your eyes and let it wash over you. Be present for the Bhog ceremony — the conclusion of the Akhand Path — the most important moment of the day.

✦ Gurudwara
Receive Karah Prasad

When sewadars come around with the warm halwa — cup both hands together (right over left) and receive it with humility. Eat immediately. Made of equal parts whole wheat flour, sugar, and ghee — three equal ingredients representing the equality of all people before the Guru. This is the Guru's blessing made edible.

✦ Seva
Langar Seva — Serve the Community

Volunteer in the langar kitchen — cooking, chopping, making chapatis, washing dishes, or serving food. This seva is considered one of the highest forms of worship in Sikhism. Arrive early and tell the sewadar you wish to do langar seva. Then eat langar sitting on the floor in Pangat (rows) with everyone — the same simple food, served with love, shared equally.

✦ Procession
Nagar Kirtan — Walk with the Guru

A grand street procession led by the Panj Pyare in blue Khalsa warrior attire — followed by the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji on a Palki Sahib, then thousands of Sangat members. Ragis sing Kirtan continuously. Gatka (Sikh martial art) is performed. Join on foot behind the Granth — walk with folded hands, sing along. As a spectator, stand and bow your head as the Granth passes.

✦ All Day
Simran — Waheguru Throughout

Whether at Gurudwara, home, or going about the day — continuously repeat "Waheguru, Waheguru, Waheguru..." inhaling on "Wahe," exhaling on "Guru." Use a Tasbih mala to count. Guru Gobind Singh Ji said Simran is the highest practice — everything else is secondary.

"Waheguru, Waheguru, Waheguru..."

📿 The Five Morning Banis — Nitnem

Bani Composed By What It Is
Japji Sahib Guru Nanak Dev Ji The foundational morning prayer — the opening composition of Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji. Covers the nature of God, purpose of human life, and the path to liberation.
Jaap Sahib Guru Gobind Singh Ji ⭐ 199 names and attributes of the divine in Sanskrit, Braj, Persian, and Arabic — God transcends all religions. Especially powerful to recite on the Guru's own Jayanti.
Tav Prasad Savaiye Guru Gobind Singh Ji ⭐ A powerful composition challenging empty ritual — declaring that without love for God in the heart, all external worship is worthless.
Chaupai Sahib Guru Gobind Singh Ji ⭐ A prayer of complete protection and surrender — asking God's hand of protection over the devotee. Said to create a divine shield. "Hamri karo haath dai rachha..."
Anand Sahib Guru Amar Das Ji 40 stanzas of divine bliss — celebrating the state of spiritual joy achieved through Naam and Sangat. The hymn of true anand (bliss).

🙏 Key Prayers & Mantras

🔱
Mool Mantar — The Root Mantra of Sikhism (Guru Nanak Dev Ji)
"Ik Onkar, Sat Naam, Karta Purakh, Nirbhau, Nirvair, Akaal Moorat, Ajooni Saibhang, Gur Prasad. Jap."
One God exists. Truth is His name. He is the Creator. Without fear. Without enmity. His form is timeless. Beyond birth and death. Self-illuminated. Realized by the Guru's grace. Meditate. — The opening of Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji. The foundation of all Sikh belief.
✨
Waheguru — The Divine Name (Simran)
"Waheguru, Waheguru, Waheguru..."
Wondrous Lord — "Wahe" is wondrous/marvelous, "Guru" is the divine enlightener. Inhale "Wahe," exhale "Guru." Repeat continuously. Guru Gobind Singh Ji said Simran is the highest practice — everything else is secondary.
⚔️
Chaupai Sahib — Prayer of Protection (Guru Gobind Singh Ji)
"Hamri karo haath dai rachha, pooran hoye chit ki ichha"
Give me Your hand and protect me. May the desires of my heart be fulfilled. The Guru's own prayer for divine protection — said to create a shield around the devotee. Recite especially on his Jayanti.
🙏
Sikh Greeting — Said at Every Meeting and Prayer's End
"Waheguru Ji Ka Khalsa, Waheguru Ji Ki Fateh"
The Khalsa belongs to the Wondrous Lord — Victory belongs to the Wondrous Lord. Given by Guru Gobind Singh Ji himself. Both people say this to each other. The battle cry and the greeting — both in one phrase.

📌 Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do's

  • Wake at Amrit Vela (before dawn) — as the Guru did every single day
  • Take full Ishnaan (bath) before prayers
  • Recite full Nitnem — especially Jaap Sahib, Chaupai Sahib & Tav Prasad Savaiye (the Guru's own compositions)
  • Visit the Gurudwara and perform Matha Tekna — bow before the Guru Granth Sahib Ji
  • Attend the Bhog ceremony — the culmination of the Akhand Path
  • Do langar seva — cook or serve food in the community kitchen
  • Eat langar sitting on the floor in Pangat with everyone
  • Do continuous Waheguru Simran throughout the day
  • Greet all Sikhs with "Waheguru Ji Ka Khalsa, Waheguru Ji Ki Fateh"

✕ Don'ts

  • Never enter the Gurudwara without covering your head
  • Never sit with feet pointing toward Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji
  • Do not consume tobacco, alcohol, or non-vegetarian food on this day
  • Do not turn your back to Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji when leaving — step back while facing the Guru
  • Do not speak loudly or use your phone during Kirtan in the Darbar Sahib
  • Do not rush through Nitnem — recite with understanding, not speed
  • Do not waste langar food — eat with humility and gratitude

He asked: "Who will give their head for the Guru?" Five men stood up. He gave them Amrit. Then he asked them to give him Amrit in return — making the Guru equal to the Sikh. That humility. That courage. That is the Khalsa. That is Guru Gobind Singh Ji.
