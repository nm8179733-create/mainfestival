📖 About

Lohri is celebrated every year on the fixed date of January 13th — the last night of the coldest month of the year, the night before Makar Sankranti. It is primarily the festival of Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, and Delhi, and wherever Punjabi communities have settled across India and the world. Unlike most Hindu festivals, it follows the solar calendar — the date never changes.

It marks the end of winter and the imminent return of the sun's warmth (Uttarayana), and is deeply connected to the Rabi harvest — the winter crop of sugarcane and mustard that reaches maturity around this time. Farmers celebrate the culmination of months of labor with gratitude, fire, music, and dance.

"Dulla Bhatti was a Robin Hood of Emperor Akbar's Punjab — he robbed the rich, gave to the poor, and most famously rescued girls from being sold into slavery and arranged their marriages with dignity. Almost every Lohri folk song is about him. His story is why Lohri is especially celebrated for newborns and newly married couples."

The first Lohri after a wedding or the birth of a child is considered especially auspicious and is celebrated with exceptional grandeur — the community pouring revdi and peanuts over the new bride or newborn like a confetti shower.

⭐ Significance

Lohri is perhaps the most viscerally joyful festival in India's calendar — where fire, food, dance, community, and the pure relief of winter ending all come together in one magnificent evening. It is a festival that says: we survived the cold, the harvest is in, our family is blessed, and tomorrow the sun comes back. Let's dance.

🎵 The Dulla Bhatti Song — Heart of Lohri

Children go door to door on Lohri day — Lohri Maangna — singing this folk song and collecting revdi, gajak, and peanuts. The same song is sung by everyone around the bonfire while circling. It is the cultural soul of Lohri — not optional entertainment but the actual ritual.

🎶 Dulla Bhatti Song — Sung Door to Door & Around the Bonfire
Sunder mundriye ho! · Tera kaun vichara ho!
Dulla Bhatti walla ho! · Dulle di dhee vyaahi ho!
Ser shakar paai ho! · Kudi da laal pathaka ho!
Kudi da saalu paata ho! · Salu kaun samete ho!
Chacha gali dese ho! · Chacha nu dena ho!
Munde nu laat maar ke! · Ik cup chaiye ho!
Meaning: "O beautiful girl! Who will think about you? Dulla Bhatti will! Dulla married off his daughter! He gave her a measure of sugar! The girl had a red shawl! The shawl slipped away! Who will gather the shawl? The uncle gives from the alley! Give something to the uncle! Kick the boy! I want one cup of tea!" — A playful folk narrative about Dulla Bhatti's generous act of marrying off a poor girl with dignity.

🗺️ Regions

Punjab
The Heart of Lohri

The fullest, grandest celebration — community bonfires in every mohalla, village-wide gatherings, Bhangra and Gidda danced with full fervor, Sarson ka Saag served from large degchis (pots), and the Dulla Bhatti song ringing through every street.

Haryana
Lohri / Lodi

Called Lodi in Haryana — the bonfire and offering traditions are the same. Families gather, circle the fire, and share revdi and til. The occasion for a newborn son's first Lohri is especially significant in Haryanvi culture.

Himachal Pradesh
Lohri

Mountain communities celebrate with bonfires against a backdrop of snow-capped peaks. The folk music and traditional Himachali songs are different from Punjab's but the fire, offering, and community gathering traditions are identical.

Delhi & Urban Diaspora
Colony Lohri

Housing societies and apartment complexes organize colony Lohri bonfires — often on terraces or open grounds. The Dhol player, Bhangra circle, and revdi sharing are brought into the urban setting with full enthusiasm.

Sindhi Community
Lal Loi

The Sindhi community celebrates Lal Loi on the same night — "Lal" means red (the fire) and "Loi" means warmth. The bonfire, offerings, and folk songs are similar with distinct Sindhi cultural elements woven in.

🎭 How to Celebrate Lohri — Step by Step

✦ Daytime
Lohri Maangna — Children Door to Door

Children go from door to door in the neighborhood singing the Dulla Bhatti song and collecting revdi, gajak, peanuts, and popcorn in a cloth bag. Each household gives generously. The collected items are brought to the evening bonfire and shared with everyone. If a household gives nothing, the children sing a playful teasing verse at their door.

✦ Afternoon
Build the Bonfire

Choose a safe open outdoor space. Arrange dry wood logs in a conical/pyramid structure — large logs at the base, smaller ones leaning inward. Fill inside with dry leaves, twigs, and kindling. Place dry cow dung cakes within and around it. At the very center top, place a long sugarcane stalk — the central axis of the fire. Keep a bucket of water or sand nearby.

✦ After Sunset
Light the Bonfire — "Lohri Aayi!"

After sunset (6–8 PM), the eldest member lights the bonfire. As the fire catches and rises, everyone folds hands and says "Jai Lohri Mata Ki!" or "Lohri Aayi, Lohri Aayi!" The whole family and neighbors gather in a circle around the fire. The warmth of the fire in the cold January night is part of the magic — it is physically and symbolically warming.

✦ Puja
Offer into the Fire — Seven Sacred Offerings

Everyone takes handfuls of the sacred Lohri foods and throws them into the fire as offerings to Agni and Surya, saying: "Aadar aye, dilather jaye" (May honor come, may poverty go). These are the harvest crops of winter — offered back to the divine in gratitude.

✦ Pradakshina
Circle the Fire 4 Times — Clockwise

After the initial offerings, everyone circles the burning bonfire 4 times clockwise (representing the four directions). As you circle, keep throwing handfuls of revdi, peanuts, and popcorn into the fire. Sing the Dulla Bhatti song as you circle. This communal circling — singing, throwing offerings, turning together — is the very heart of Lohri.

✦ Special
Pehli Lohri — Newborns & New Brides

If there is a newborn in the family — the baby is brought to the fire and held while circling, blessings sought for their life. If there is a newly married couple — they circle the fire together holding hands or connected by her dupatta pallu, echoing the seven pheras of their wedding. The family then showers them with revdi and peanuts poured over their heads — a blessing confetti shower.

✦ Celebration
Bhangra & Gidda — Dance!

After the puja and circling, the dhol begins to beat and the dancing starts. Men dance Bhangra — vigorous, full-body, arms raised, spinning in rhythm. Women dance Gidda — clapping, spinning, calling out witty Boliyan verses while others respond. You don't need to know the steps. Just clap, move, and join in. Nobody sits on the sidelines if they can help it.

✦ Feast
Sarson ka Saag & Makki ki Roti

The soul food of Lohri — slow-cooked mustard greens finished with white butter (makhan) on top, eaten with thick corn flour flatbreads on a cold January night around the bonfire. One of the most perfect food experiences in all of Indian cuisine. Also pass around roasted peanuts, revdi, gajak — shared through the evening while the fire burns.

✦ Gifts
Share with Neighbors & Give Lohri Thali

During the day, send plates of revdi, gajak, peanuts, and sweets to all neighbors. For a new bride's first Lohri, the in-laws present her a formal Lohri Thali of revdi, gajak, peanuts, and dry fruits — welcoming her into the family. For newborns, extended family visits to bless the child with clothes, gold, and sweets.

✦ Next Morning
Maghi — Makar Sankranti Morning

The morning after Lohri is Maghi — the Punjabi Makar Sankranti. Wake up early and take a dip in a river or add a few drops of gangajal to your bath. Visit the Gurudwara, offer prayers, and participate in community langar. Maghi is also associated with the Chhote Sahibzade — the four young sons of Guru Gobind Singh Ji — and is a day of remembrance and gratitude.

🌾 The Seven Sacred Offerings to Agni

These are the harvest foods of the Rabi season — offered back to the divine fire as thanksgiving. Say "Aadar aye, dilather jaye" with each offering.

🍬 Revdi
Sesame & sugar candy — the most essential Lohri item, both offered and eaten
🍫 Gajak
Sesame & jaggery brittle — the harvest's sweetness given back to the fire
🥜 Peanuts
Roasted groundnuts — inseparable from Lohri, thrown in by the handful
🍿 Popcorn
Makki / phuliye — the corn harvest crackling joyfully in the fire
🌿 Sesame / Til
Sacred at both Lohri and Sankranti — the winter seed crop offered with reverence
🎋 Sugarcane
The Rabi crop itself — placed in the fire and also eaten fresh around it
🟫 Jaggery
Gur — the sweetened form of the sugarcane harvest, offered as a lump

🙏 Prayers & Folk Chants

🔥
Offering Prayer — Said with Every Handful into the Fire
"Aadar aye, dilather jaye"
May honor and prosperity come — may poverty go. Said with both hands as you throw each offering of revdi, gajak, peanuts, sesame, and sugarcane into the fire. The most essential Lohri utterance.
🌾
Circling Chant — Said While Doing Pradakshina
"Lohri di lakh lakh vadhai, sada ghar vasda rahe"
A hundred thousand congratulations on Lohri — may our home always be prosperous and inhabited. Chanted while walking clockwise around the bonfire. "Vasda rahe" — may it keep living — is one of the most beautiful blessings in Punjabi.
👶
Blessing for Newborns — Said by Elders
"Jug jug jiyo, chardikala vich raho"
Live long, remain in high spirits always. "Chardikala" — perpetual high spirits, optimism, and divine energy — is one of the most important concepts in Punjabi and Sikh culture. This is the blessing for the new life being celebrated.
🎶
Lighting the Fire — Said as the Bonfire Catches
"Jai Lohri Mata Ki!" / "Lohri Aayi, Lohri Aayi!"
Victory to Lohri Mata! / Lohri has come, Lohri has come! The joyful exclamation at the moment the bonfire blazes to life — said with folded hands and a full heart. The whole gathering says it together.

📌 Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do's

  • Light the bonfire after sunset in a safe open outdoor space
  • Offer all seven items — revdi, gajak, peanuts, popcorn, sesame, sugarcane, jaggery — with intention and the prayer
  • Circle the bonfire clockwise 4 times while singing the Dulla Bhatti song
  • Perform the special Pehli Lohri rituals for newborns and newly married couples
  • Dance Bhangra and Gidda with full energy — Lohri without dancing is incomplete
  • Eat Sarson ka Saag and Makki ki Roti around the fire
  • Share revdi, gajak, and peanuts with all neighbors on the day
  • Keep a bucket of water or sand near the bonfire at all times
  • Wake the next morning for the Maghi bath and Gurudwara visit

✕ Don'ts

  • Never light the bonfire indoors or in any enclosed space
  • Do not let the bonfire become uncontrolled — size matters for safety
  • Do not leave children unsupervised near the fire
  • Do not offer carelessly — throw with both hands and intention, not absentmindedly
  • Do not skip the folk songs — they are not entertainment, they are the ritual
  • Do not let anyone sit on the sidelines if they can join in dancing

The fire burns on the coldest night of the year. The harvest is in. The family is together. The dhol is beating. Tomorrow the sun turns north and warmth returns. Tonight — we dance.
