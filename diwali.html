WHAT IS DIWALI

The Festival of Lights

Diwali — from Deepavali, meaning "row of lights" — is the most widely celebrated Hindu festival in the world. It is a 5-day festival observed during the new moon (Amavasya) of the Hindu month of Kartika, falling in October or November. In 2026, it falls around October 17–21. Check drikpanchang.com for the exact Lakshmi Puja Muhurta — the most important timing of the entire festival.

The main Diwali night falls on Amavasya — the darkest night of the lunar month. Yet it is precisely on this night that millions of diyas are lit across India and around the world, transforming the darkest night into the brightest.

The festival celebrates multiple simultaneous events across different traditions — Lord Rama's return to Ayodhya, Goddess Lakshmi's birth from the cosmic ocean, Krishna's victory over Narakasura, Lord Mahavira's attainment of Moksha (for Jains), and Guru Hargobind Ji's release from Mughal imprisonment — Bandi Chhor Divas for Sikhs.

The true heart of Diwali is the Lakshmi Puja performed on the main night — a deeply devotional act of inviting the goddess of prosperity, beauty, and abundance into the home. Every diya lit is an invitation. She enters only the brightest, cleanest home.

STORIES & ORIGINS

Why We Celebrate

🏹
Hindu — Pan India
Lord Rama's Return to Ayodhya

After 14 years of exile and the defeat of the demon king Ravana, Lord Rama returned home on this Amavasya night. The people of Ayodhya lit thousands of rows of diyas to welcome him — this is the origin of Diwali's defining tradition of light.

🌸
Hindu — Goddess of Prosperity
Birth of Goddess Lakshmi

Lakshmi emerged from the Samudra Manthan (cosmic ocean churning) on this night and is believed to roam the earth on Diwali night blessing homes that are clean, lit, and welcoming. She enters only the brightest, cleanest home — every diya lit is an invitation to her.

🐚
Hindu — Krishna Tradition
Krishna's Victory Over Narakasura

Lord Krishna killed the demon Narakasura on the day before Diwali (Naraka Chaturdashi), liberating 16,000 women he had imprisoned. People celebrated with oil baths — observed today as the Abhyanga Snanam tradition of Choti Diwali.

☸️
Jain Tradition
Mahavira's Nirvana

For Jains, Diwali is the day Lord Mahavira attained Moksha — liberation from the cycle of birth and death. The ultimate light of the soul extinguishing all karma. Jains observe Diwali with prayers, fasting, and lighting lamps in memory of the soul's eternal light.

🪯
Sikh Tradition — Bandi Chhor Divas
Guru Hargobind Ji's Release

For Sikhs, Diwali is Bandi Chhor Divas — the day the sixth Sikh Guru was released from Mughal imprisonment along with 52 other kings. The Golden Temple at Amritsar is illuminated spectacularly on this night in one of the most breathtaking celebrations in India.

💫
Universal Meaning
Light Over Darkness

Across every tradition, Diwali carries the same universal message: light always overcomes darkness, knowledge always overcomes ignorance, and the smallest flame lit with devotion on the darkest night changes everything.

THE FIVE SACRED DAYS

The Journey of Deepavali

Day 1 — Dhanteras

🪙 Dhanteras — Dhanvantari's Day

Dhantrayodashi — the 13th lunar day, two days before the main Diwali night. "Dhan" means wealth and "Teras" means thirteenth. On this day Dhanvantari — the divine physician and God of Ayurveda — emerged from the Samudra Manthan carrying the pot of Amrit and the knowledge of medicine. Yama (the God of Death) is also appeased on this day.

  • 1Buy gold, silver, or metal items — even a small silver coin or steel vessel fulfills this tradition. Lakshmi enters your home through every auspicious purchase made on this day.
  • 2Dhanvantari Puja — light a diya and offer flowers, praying for the health of every family member. His mantra: "Om Namo Bhagavate Vasudevaya Dhanvantaraye Amrita Kalasha Hastaya Sarva Amaya Vinashanaya Trailokya Nathaya Sri Maha Vishnave Namaha"
  • 3Light 13 diyas — one for each Teras — placing them at the doorstep, in every room, and in the kitchen. The kitchen diya on Dhanteras is specifically important as the kitchen is Lakshmi's domain.
  • 4Yamadeepa — light one diya facing south (Yama's direction) and pray for protection from untimely death for all family members. This is called Yamadeepa and is a Dhanteras-specific ritual.
Day 2 — Choti Diwali

🌙 Choti Diwali — Naraka Chaturdashi

The day before the main Diwali night. Celebrates Krishna's defeat of the demon Narakasura before sunrise, liberating 16,000 women he had imprisoned. The next morning people celebrated with oil baths before dawn — this is observed today as Abhyanga Snanam.

  • 1Wake before sunrise — this is essential. The Abhyanga Snanam (oil bath) must be taken before the sun rises. Apply warm sesame or coconut oil all over the body and hair, massage thoroughly, then bathe with warm water. Taking this bath before sunrise connects you to that ancient celebration of liberation.
  • 2Wear new clothes after bathing — this is the first wearing of Diwali new clothes for many families.
  • 3Light 14 diyas in the evening, placed in every corner of the home — traditionally 14 diyas for Naraka Chaturdashi.
Day 3 — The Grand Night

🪔 Diwali Amavasya — The Most Sacred Night

The most sacred, most joyful, most magnificent night of the entire festival. Check the exact Lakshmi Puja Muhurta on drikpanchang.com for your city — the Pradosh Kaal window (typically 6–8 PM) is when Lakshmi is most actively present and receptive.

Morning: Clean the home one final time. Make fresh rangoli at the entrance — the most elaborate and beautiful of the year. Traditional motifs include lotus flowers, diyas, Lakshmi's feet (auspicious footprints leading into the home), swastikas, and geometric patterns.

Draw Lakshmi's Footprints — small footprints made with rice flour paste or rangoli powder from the entrance toward the puja room, pointing inward. You are literally marking the path for the goddess to walk into your home. This tradition is deeply moving.

Setting Up the Altar: Drape the altar with red cloth. Place new Lakshmi and Ganesha idols — buy new ones every Diwali, representing fresh invitations to the divine. Ganesha on the left, Lakshmi on the right. Place new silver/gold coin, account books (Bahi Khata), lotus flowers, coins and currency, all jewelry, kaju katli, fruits, and panchamritam before the idols.

The Lakshmi Puja — Complete Process:

  • 1Ganesh Puja first — always. Offer flowers and akshat. Chant "Om Gam Ganapataye Namaha" 11 times. No puja begins without Ganesha.
  • 2Kalash Puja — worship the filled Kalash with Gangajal, mango leaves, and coconut. The Kalash represents the cosmic ocean from which Lakshmi emerged.
  • 3Lakshmi Abhishekam — pour Panchamritam gently over the Lakshmi idol while chanting "Om Shri Mahalakshmyai Namaha". Then bathe with Gangajal. Dry the idol. Apply kumkum on the forehead. Apply chandan. Offer fresh lotus flowers — Lakshmi's most sacred flower.
  • 4Offer saffron and gold — Lakshmi specifically loves saffron (kesar) and gold-colored items. Offer a pinch of saffron mixed in milk over the idol.
  • 5Chant the Lakshmi Mantra 108 times: "Om Shri Mahalakshmyai Namaha" — the primary mantra of the entire festival.
  • 6Recite Shri Sukta — the most important Vedic hymn to Lakshmi, from the Rig Veda, 16 verses. Reciting or listening to Shri Sukta on Diwali night is one of the most powerful acts of the festival. Available on YouTube in full recitation.
  • 7Lakshmi Aarti — wave camphor clockwise with full voice and devotion: "Om Jai Lakshmi Mata, Maiya Jai Lakshmi Mata, Tumko Nishdin Sevat, Hari Vishnu Vidhata..."
  • 8Offer Kaju Katli and sweets as naivedyam. Kaju katli is specifically Lakshmi's favorite sweet.
  • 9Chopda Puja — if you run a business or keep accounts: open the new account book, write "Shri" at the top as the first word, and place it before Lakshmi asking for prosperity and integrity in your financial dealings throughout the coming year.
  • 10Prostrate completely and pray for Lakshmi's blessings — not just for material wealth but for the abundance of health, happiness, wisdom, and love in your home.

Lighting the Diyas: After puja — as night falls — light every single diya. Place them in rows along every windowsill, on every step, along every balcony railing, at the entrance, in the puja room, in the kitchen, in every room, in the garden. One diya faces outward at the front door to guide Lakshmi from the street. The principle: no darkness anywhere.

Sweet Distribution: On Diwali night take plates of sweets door to door wishing everyone "Diwali ki Shubhkamnaayen." This community sharing of sweets on the night of maximum light is one of the most beautiful social traditions of India.

Day 4 — Govardhan Puja

🏔 Govardhan Puja — Annakut

Celebrates Krishna lifting the Govardhan hill on his finger to shelter the people of Vrindavan from Indra's devastating rains — an act of protecting his community against the arrogance of power. A small hill of food — Annakut (mountain of food) — is made from 56 different food items (Chappan Bhog) and offered to Krishna, then distributed as prasad to the entire community.

In Maharashtra this day is celebrated as Padwa / Pratipada — the first day of the new year in the Vikrama calendar. Husbands give gifts to wives and the marital bond is honored and celebrated.

Day 5 — Bhai Dooj

💝 Bhai Dooj — The Bond Between Siblings

The final day of Diwali — also called Bhai Phota or Yama Dwitiya. Yama (God of Death) visited his sister Yamuna on this day. She welcomed him with great love — applied tilak on his forehead, fed him, and prayed for his wellbeing. Yama was so moved he declared: any brother who receives his sister's tilak on this day will be freed from the fear of death.

Unlike Raksha Bandhan where the sister asks for protection, Bhai Dooj is the sister giving protection — her love and prayer is the shield against death for her brother. Sisters apply tilak (kumkum, chandan, and rice) on brothers' foreheads and pray for their long life. Brothers give gifts. The family eats together.

TRADITIONAL FOODS

Tastes of Diwali

Most Iconic · Lakshmi's Favourite
Kaju Katli

Diamond-shaped cashew and sugar fudge with edible silver vark (leaf). Lakshmi's favorite offering and the most gifted sweet of the entire Diwali season — distributed to neighbors, family, and colleagues in silver-foil boxes. The taste of Diwali in one diamond-shaped bite.

North India Favourite
Motichoor Laddoo

Tiny golden pearls of gram flour fried and bound into perfect spheres of sweetness with sugar syrup and aromatic cardamom. Distributed by the hundreds on Diwali night and offered in large quantities to Lakshmi as naivedyam.

Festival Classic
Gulab Jamun

Soft milk-solid dumplings deep-fried to a rich golden brown and soaked in rose-flavored sugar syrup — served warm at the Diwali feast and offered as divine naivedyam. The fragrance of hot gulab jamun in sugar syrup is the fragrance of celebration.

Diwali Essential
Jalebi

Pretzel-shaped spirals of fermented batter deep-fried and immediately dipped in saffron-infused sugar syrup. Crispy on the outside, syrup-laden inside — utterly festive and impossible to eat just one. Sold in huge quantities at every sweet shop during Diwali season.

South & West India
Chakli / Murukku

Crispy spiral savory snack made from rice flour and spices — a Diwali essential in Maharashtra (Chakli) and South India (Murukku). Made at home in large quantities weeks before Diwali and packed into tins for gifting and sharing.

North India Staple
Mathri

Flaky, buttery, deep-fried savory crackers spiced with ajwain and black pepper — eaten with mango pickle as the quintessential North Indian Diwali snack. Made days in advance and stored in tins; the go-to accompaniment for tea during the Diwali season.

Homemade Favourite
Besan Laddoo

Gram flour slowly roasted in ghee until fragrant and golden, then mixed with powdered sugar and cardamom and shaped into round laddoos. The warmth of ghee-roasted gram flour is the fragrance of Diwali kitchens across India — a homemade sweetness impossible to buy.

Corporate & Family Gifting
Premium Dry Fruit Boxes

Gifting elegant boxes of cashews, almonds, pistachios, raisins, dried figs, and saffron to family, friends, and colleagues is one of the most important Diwali gifting traditions. Diwali is India's biggest gifting season — and dry fruit boxes are its most auspicious offering.

RITUALS & TRADITIONS

Sacred Practices

🏠
Diwali Safai — Deep Cleaning

In the weeks before Diwali, every home undergoes the most thorough deep cleaning of the year. Every corner, shelf, and piece of furniture is cleaned. Old, broken, and unused items are discarded. This is not merely housekeeping — it is spiritual preparation. Lakshmi represents order, beauty, and abundance; a clean home already expresses her qualities and she recognizes herself in it.

👣
Drawing Lakshmi's Footprints

Small footprints are drawn with rice flour paste from the entrance toward the puja room, pointing inward — inviting Lakshmi to walk into your home. This tradition is deeply moving: you are literally marking the path for the goddess to enter. One of the most beautiful and intimate rituals of the entire festival.

📒
Chopda Puja — Blessing the Books

Businesspeople place their Bahi Khata (account books) before Lakshmi on Diwali night. The new account book is opened, the first word written is "Shri" as a blessing, and the first entry is made. This tradition — especially important in Gujarat and Rajasthan — invites Lakshmi's blessing into every financial transaction of the coming year.

🛁
Abhyanga Snanam — The Oil Bath

On Choti Diwali (Day 2), waking before sunrise for the ritual oil bath is essential. Warm sesame or coconut oil is applied all over the body and hair, massaged in deeply, then bathed off with warm water before the sun rises. This pre-dawn ritual connects you to Krishna's ancient celebration of Narakasura's defeat — liberation enacted in the body.

🍬
Sweet Distribution — Door to Door

On Diwali night, families take plates of sweets and go door to door wishing everyone "Diwali ki Shubhkamnaayen." This community sharing of sweets on the night of maximum light is one of the most beautiful social traditions in all of India — a gesture of abundance freely shared with every neighbor.

🪙
Dhanteras Shopping

Purchasing gold, silver, or new utensils on Dhanteras is considered extremely auspicious — even buying a small silver coin fulfills this tradition. Lakshmi is said to enter your home through new purchases made on this day. Diwali is India's biggest shopping season and Dhanteras marks its most sacred beginning.

🎨
Rangoli at the Entrance

The most elaborate rangoli of the year is made at the entrance on Diwali morning. Traditional motifs include lotus flowers (Lakshmi's sacred flower), diyas, Lakshmi's footprints, swastikas (ancient symbol of auspiciousness), and geometric patterns in vibrant colors. Rangoli is a visual welcome mat for the goddess herself.

🪔
No Darkness Anywhere

The guiding principle of Diwali lighting is absolute: no darkness anywhere in the home. Diyas are placed on every windowsill, every step, every balcony railing, in every room, in the kitchen, in the puja room, in the garden. One diya faces outward at the front door — to guide Lakshmi toward your home from the street itself.

SACRED MANTRAS & PRAYERS

Words of Light

Primary Lakshmi Mantra · Chant 108 Times During Puja
"Om Shri Mahalakshmyai Namaha"
I bow to the Great Goddess Lakshmi — source of all abundance, beauty, prosperity, and grace. This is the most important mantra of Diwali, chanted 108 times during the Lakshmi puja. The number 108 is sacred in Hindu tradition.
Shri Sukta Opening Verse · Rig Veda — Most Powerful Diwali Recitation
"Hiranya Varnaam Harineem, Suvarna Rajata Srajaam
Chandraam Hiranmayeem Lakshmeem, Jaatavedo Ma Aavaha"
Meaning: "O Agni — bring to me Lakshmi who is golden complexioned, who wears garlands of gold and silver, who is radiant like the moon." Reciting or listening to all 16 verses of Shri Sukta on Diwali night is one of the most powerful acts of the festival. Available on YouTube — search "Shri Sukta full recitation."
Lakshmi Gayatri Mantra
"Om Mahadevyai Cha Vidmahe, Vishnu Patnyai Cha Dheemahi
Tanno Lakshmi Prachodayat"
Meaning: We meditate on the great goddess, consort of Vishnu — may Lakshmi illuminate and inspire us toward abundance and wisdom.
Kubera Mantra · God of Wealth — Worshipped Alongside Lakshmi on Diwali
"Om Yakshaya Kuberaya Vaishravanaya Dhana Dhanyadi Pataye
Dhana Dhanya Samriddhim Me Dehi Dapaya Swaha"
Meaning: O Kubera — Lord of Yakshas, son of Vishrava, master of wealth and grain — grant me abundance of wealth and grain. Kubera is the treasurer of the Gods and is specifically worshipped alongside Lakshmi on Diwali night for material abundance.
Lakshmi Aarti — Sung While Waving Camphor Flame
"Om Jai Lakshmi Mata, Maiya Jai Lakshmi Mata
Tumko Nishdin Sevat, Hari Vishnu Vidhata
Om Jai Lakshmi Mata"
The most beloved Lakshmi aarti — sung while waving a lit camphor flame clockwise before the goddess idol. Sing with full voice and devotion. The sound and the flame together are the offering.
Dhanvantari Mantra · For Dhanteras — Praying for Health of All Family Members
"Om Namo Bhagavate Vasudevaya Dhanvantaraye
Amrita Kalasha Hastaya Sarva Amaya Vinashanaya
Trailokya Nathaya Sri Maha Vishnave Namaha"
Meaning: O Lord Dhanvantari — holding the pot of immortal nectar, destroyer of all diseases, lord of the three worlds, the great Vishnu — I bow to you. Chanted on Dhanteras while lighting the diya, praying specifically for the health and healing of every family member.
Diwali Greeting Prayer
"Deepavali ki Subhkamnaayein —
Aapke Jeevan mein Sukh, Samridhi, aur Prakash bana rahe"
Meaning: Diwali greetings — may happiness, prosperity, and light remain in your life always. The traditional greeting shared door to door when distributing sweets on Diwali night.
GUIDE

Do's & Don'ts

✅ Essential Do's
  • 🏠Deep clean the entire home before Diwali — every corner, without exception
  • 🛕Buy new Lakshmi and Ganesha idols specifically for this year's puja — fresh invitation
  • ⏰Check and strictly follow the Lakshmi Puja Muhurta on drikpanchang.com — timing is everything
  • 👣Draw Lakshmi's footprints from entrance to puja room — invite her to walk in
  • 🪔Light maximum diyas — no darkness anywhere in the home
  • 📖Recite Shri Sukta during Lakshmi puja — the most powerful Vedic offering of the night
  • 📒Open new account book (Chopda Puja) if you run a business — write "Shri" first
  • 🍬Distribute sweets to neighbors on Diwali night — go door to door
  • 🛁Take oil bath before sunrise on Choti Diwali — this timing is essential
  • 🪙Buy gold or silver on Dhanteras — even one small coin fulfills the tradition
  • 💝Observe Bhai Dooj on the fifth day — the sister's tilak completes the festival
  • ♻️Use eco-friendly / green crackers if observing the firecracker tradition
❌ Important Don'ts
  • ⏱Do not perform Lakshmi Puja outside the Muhurta window if avoidable — the timing window is sacred
  • 🏭Do not use Plaster of Paris or chemically painted diyas — use only natural clay diyas
  • 💨Do not use excessive firecrackers — be deeply considerate of health, animals, and environment
  • 🃏Do not gamble on Diwali — though common, it is not part of the authentic spiritual tradition
  • 📱Do not let Diwali become only about shopping — the puja, diyas, and community are its true heart
  • 🚪Do not skip the sweet distribution — going door to door with sweets is one of India's most beautiful human traditions
  • 🕯️Do not leave diyas unattended near curtains or flammable materials — fire safety is non-negotiable
  • 🌑Do not leave any room, corner, or space in darkness — Lakshmi does not enter dark places
  • 🐾Do not light firecrackers near animals, elderly people, or those with respiratory conditions
  • 🧹Do not perform Lakshmi Puja in an uncleaned space — prepare the home and altar first

"Diwali is the festival that chose the darkest night of the year to light the most lights. It did not wait for an easier night. It chose Amavasya — complete darkness — and said: this is exactly when we light every lamp we have. Because that is what light is for. Not for when it is already bright. But for the darkest night. For the most difficult moment. For the place where darkness feels most permanent. That is when you light a diya."
